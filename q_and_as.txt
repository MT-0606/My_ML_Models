Q: What feature, or column, do the DataFrames have in common?
A: The `business_id` feature. This makes sense, because each review, tip, photo, etc needs to be traced to each particular restaurant.

Q: What features best correlate, both positively and negatively, with Yelp rating?
A: The `average_review_sentiment` (positive) and `average_review_length` (negative) features.

Q: Why do you think `average_review_sentiment` correlates so well with Yelp rating?
A: It could have something to do with the fact that how the restaurant made the reviews feel influenced their decision to rate it high or low.

Q: What do these R^2 values say about our model? Do you think these features alone are able to effectively predict Yelp ratings?
A: Both R^2 values are vary low (approximately 0.08). Because they are far closer to zero than to one, the created model does not fit our data very well. Therefore, the selected features (`average_review_length` and `average_review_age`) cannot effectively predict Yelp ratings.

Q: For a perfect linear regression model we would expect to see the data plotted along the line y = x, indicating homoscedasticity. Is this the case? If not, why not? Would you call this model heteroscedastic or homoscedastic?
A: There is no such plot in our resulting graph. Therefore, this particular model is heteroscedastic.

Q: How does changing the feature sets affect the model's R^2 value? Which features are most important to predicting Yelp rating in the different models? Which models appear more or less homoscedastic?
A: `average_review_sentiment` (the sentiment feature set) is the only singular feature to be homoscedastic. Numeric features are the most homoscedastic, while binary features are the least homoscedastic.
